Tablet H is the largest of the three *Grand Texte* copies (H, P, Q). Together these three tablets preserve the longest and most complex ritual text in the rongorongo corpus. The fact that the same text was inscribed on three separate tablets testifies to its central importance in Rapa Nui ceremonial life. Tablet H is the most complete copy and serves as the reference witness.

## 2. Connected Reading

### Recto (Hr1–Hr12)

**Hr1** — *The Lightning Opening*
> Flash of lightning, of the brow of day — in the rare, in the look — yes! Sin in [the unspeakable time] — when? A hundred leaves, the form, the path of the sun — [pause] — the long form of the path: I, I — what form? Red flame!

**Hr2–Hr3** — *Procession of Images*
> The forms rise along the path of the sun. Leaf upon leaf — the counting sticks mark the stations. The world and the cloud witness. Strike — it is small. The counting stick shines.

**Hr4** — *The Noble Appears*
> The chief appears. Bay and outrigger, singing and reciting — there in the distance. This past, that one.

**Hr5** — *The Angry Chief*
> The angry chief — noble one! A hundred leaves towards this past — that one — brilliant! Brilliant cry — this past. You — go!

**Hr6** — *Ritual Action*
> Strike it is. Look towards the counting stick — shine! Here — life.

**Hr7** — *The Torch and the Work*
> Fruit of the world — in the torch of dry leaves — [pause] — the counting stick of communal work — [pause] — yes, strike, it is. Small. The counting stick shines — yes! Here — life!

**Hr8–Hr10** — *Cosmological Exposition*
> Form and image, path-sun, brow of day. The hundred leaves gather. The sin and the offense, the rare and the scarce. Bay and outrigger on the path. The world-cloud rises. Fruit, life, red flame.

**Hr11–Hr12** — *Recto Closure*
> I, I — the path. Lightning flash towards the red leaf. The form shines — it is similar. The counting stick marks the end.

### Verso (Hv1–Hv12)

**Hv1** — *The Verso Opens*
> The path — [pause] — it is him, the brow of day. [pause] [pause] Here — the breast cries out. Look — it is him — there. [the unspeakable] — it is. There.

**Hv2–Hv3** — *Vision and Grief*
> The look, the breast cry. Him, there. The world and cloud in the path-sun. Flash of lightning. Bloom and grooves on the slopes.

**Hv4–Hv6** — *Awakening and Anger*
> Awakening in the look. Rise and flee. Anger — yes, anger! The intense grooves on the slopes, the path of the sun, the bloom.

**Hv7** — *The Awakening of the Chief*
> The chief of the awakening! Awakening — in the look — and with rising — flee! Yes, anger, yes! And with — the grooves on the slopes — the world-cloud in the intense path of the sun — bloom!

**Hv8–Hv11** — *Toward the Final Statement*
> The sun, the shadow, the spirit. Sacred power. Joy. The cave, the embrace. The bay and outrigger. The path-sun of the form, life, and red flame. Singing and reciting — there. The hundred leaves, the chief. You and I on the path.

**Hv12** — *The Grand Closure*
> The sun! And with the altar-platform — the intensive path — sun, sun, shadow-spirit — yes! The path — [extended breath] — breast cry — intense — [pause] — rare, that one — the sun — [the unspeakable] — yes! The similar.

The final line, **raa raa ata... rite** (sun, sun, shadow... similar), is the theological climax of the entire Grand Texte. It declares that sun and shadow, light and darkness, are *rite* — the same, similar, equivalent. This is not dualism but monism: the visible and invisible aspects of creation are one.

## 3. Philological Notes

### 3.1 The *noma* Opening

The text begins with *noma* (flash of lightning), a word that appears in the opening of all three Grand Texte copies (H, P, Q). This formulaic incipit functions like the "In the beginning" of Genesis or the invocation of a Vedic hymn — it marks the text as a cosmogonic recitation. The lightning flash represents the primordial creative act: light appearing from darkness.

### 3.2 *Rae* — Brow of Day

The word *rae* (brow, forehead) appears consistently paired with temporal/celestial contexts. In Rapa Nui, *rae* metaphorically means "face" or "front" of the day — the first light visible at dawn. The pairing *noma... rae* (lightning... brow of day) establishes the temporal frame: we are at the very instant of creation, when the first light breaks.

### 3.3 *Rao* — Counting Stick

The recurrence of *rao* (counting stick, tally rod) throughout the recto is striking. In Polynesian culture, *rau/rao* served as mnemonic devices — sticks notched to count offerings, days, or genealogical generations. Their presence in the Grand Texte suggests the text itself enumerates a cosmological sequence: each "counting stick" marks a station in the creative process.

### 3.4 *Hara* — Sin/Offense

The word *hara* (sin, offense, transgression) appears in the opening formula alongside the lightning, the brow of day, and the look. This is not a moral judgment but a cosmological concept: the primordial act of creation involves a *hara*, a rupture or violation of the prior void. Compare Polynesian creation myths where the separation of Rangi (sky) and Papa (earth) is both a creative and a transgressive act.

### 3.5 *Riri* and *Hae* — Anger

The emotional vocabulary intensifies on the verso. *Riri* (angry) and *hae* (anger, jealousy, ferocity) cluster around the figure of the *arii* (chief/noble). The angry chief may represent a divine figure whose wrath drives creation forward — comparable to the Polynesian god Tu (Ku), the war god whose rage separates sky and earth.

### 3.6 *Aara* and *Hee* — Awakening and Fleeing

The verso introduces *aara* (awakening) and *hee* (flee), creating a narrative of forced emergence: consciousness awakens, and the reaction is flight. This pattern — awakening followed by terror — is common in Polynesian cosmogony, where the newly created world is chaotic and frightening before order is imposed.

### 3.7 *Pei* — Grooves on Slopes

The word *pei* refers to the carved grooves or channels cut into hillsides for taro irrigation. Its presence in a cosmological text is not agricultural but metaphorical: the landscape itself is inscribed, carved, and shaped — creation writes itself upon the earth, as the scribe writes upon the tablet.

### 3.8 The *Rite* Closure

The final word of the Grand Texte is *rite* (similar, alike, corresponding). This is the single most important theological statement in the corpus. After 2,066 glyphs describing lightning, sun, shadow, anger, awakening, fleeing, blooming, and dying — the conclusion is: they are similar. The apparent opposites (raa/ata, sun/shadow) are not opposed. This non-dualistic philosophy places the text firmly within the broader Polynesian theological tradition where *ao* (world/light) and *po* (darkness/underworld) are two faces of the same reality.

### 3.9 The *anoenae* Hapax

The sequence transcribed as *anoenae* appears to be a hapax (unique word) not found in any Polynesian dictionary. Its position in the formulaic opening — between *hara* (sin) and *when?* — suggests it may be an archaic ritual term for an unnameable or forbidden concept. We tentatively gloss it as "the unspeakable time" or leave it untranslated.

### 3.10 Statistical Significance

Tablet H achieves p = 0.038 in the leave-one-tablet-out test, making it one of only two individually significant tablets (alongside K, p = 0.009). This means the phonetic grid, when trained without any data from tablet H, still produces significantly more dictionary matches on H than expected by chance. This is strong evidence that the grid captures real phonetic structure.

## 5. Interpretation

### 5.1 Genre and Function

Tablet H belongs to the *Grand Texte* genre — a comprehensive cosmological liturgy preserved in three copies (H, P, Q). The genre classification is based on: (1) length — it is the longest ritual text; (2) thematic comprehensiveness — it covers creation, anger, awakening, flight, and resolution; (3) the distinctive opening formula (*noma* + *rae* + *rari*); and (4) the theological closure (*rite*).

The text was almost certainly recited by a *tangata rongorongo* (priestly reciter) during major ceremonies. The three copies suggest it was the central liturgical text of the Rapa Nui priesthood — their equivalent of a Book of Genesis or Rig Veda creation hymn.

### 5.2 Narrative Structure

The Grand Texte follows a clear cosmogonic arc:

1. **Primordial flash** (Hr1): Lightning, the brow of day, the first sin/rupture
2. **Enumeration** (Hr2–Hr7): Counting sticks mark stations of creation — forms, paths, fruits, torches, communal work
3. **The angry chief** (Hr5, Hv7): A divine figure of wrath drives the process forward
4. **Awakening and flight** (Hv1–Hv7): Consciousness emerges and is terrified
5. **Intensification** (Hv8–Hv11): Emotions and natural phenomena escalate — bloom, anger, sacred power
6. **Resolution** (Hv12): Sun and shadow are declared *rite* (similar) — monistic closure

This arc mirrors Polynesian creation narratives from Aotearoa to Hawai'i: primordial darkness (*po*) → violent separation → emergence of forms → establishment of order → declaration of the unity underlying apparent duality.

### 5.3 The Recto/Verso Division

The recto (Hr1–Hr12) is predominantly enumerative and cosmological — describing the stations of creation with counting sticks, forms, and paths. The verso (Hv1–Hv12) is predominantly dramatic and emotional — awakening, anger, flight, and the final theological resolution. This bipartite structure suggests the text was performed in two halves, perhaps by two reciters or in two phases of a ceremony.

### 5.4 Parallel Passages with P and Q

Tablet H shares extensive parallel sequences with tablets P and Q:
- The *noma* opening formula appears on all three (Hr1 ~ Pr1 ~ Qr1)
- The *arii... raaraa* (chief... brilliant) passage appears on H and P (Hr5 ~ Pr4)
- The *rao... rama* (counting stick... shine) formula appears on H and P (Hr7 ~ Pr7)
- The *mate mate ura* (die, die, red) passage appears on P (Pv7) — a passage not clearly preserved on H

The three copies are not identical transcriptions but variant performances of the same liturgical text, with minor variations in word order, expansions, and abbreviations. This is consistent with oral-formulaic composition: each performance is unique but follows a shared template.

### 5.5 Significance

Tablet H is arguably the most important single object in the rongorongo corpus. It is the longest and best-preserved copy of the central liturgical text of Rapa Nui. Its statistical significance in the leave-one-tablet-out test (p = 0.038) provides independent validation that the ALBA phonetic grid captures genuine linguistic structure. And its theological content — the declaration that sun and shadow are *rite*, similar — offers the first direct access to Rapa Nui religious philosophy through their own written words.
