## 1. Metadata

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Barthel designation | P |
| Common name | Grand de Saint-Petersbourg |
| Location | Peter the Great Museum of Anthropology and Ethnography (Kunstkamera), St. Petersburg |
| Total glyphs | 1,993 |
| Lines | 22 (recto Pr1–Pr11, verso Pv1–Pv11) |
| ALBA coverage | 91.7% |
| Writing direction | Boustrophedon (reverse boustrophedon) |
| Genre | *Grand Texte* — comprehensive cosmological liturgy (second copy) |

Tablet P is the second of the three *Grand Texte* copies (H, P, Q). With 1,993 glyphs it is slightly shorter than H (2,066) but longer than Q (1,169). It is the only Grand Texte copy held outside Chile (in Russia, acquired through the Miklukho-Maklay collection). Its coverage (91.7%) is marginally lower than H's (93.6%), likely due to scribal variants or allographic differences in the St. Petersburg scribal tradition.

## 2. Connected Reading

### Recto (Pr1–Pr11)

**Pr1** — *The Lightning Opening (Variant)*
> In the flash of lightning — the result of the living — in the rare — a hundred leaves — in [the cry] — yes! — [the unspeakable time] — when? A hundred leaves, the form, the path of the sun — there — I, of I — the result of the form — lightning flash towards the red leaf!

**Pr2–Pr3** — *Forms and Stations*
> The forms on the path-sun. The counting sticks shine. Bay and outrigger — singing and reciting — there, in the distance.

**Pr4** — *The Noble Arrives*
> Yes — bay and outrigger — of singing and reciting — there, of this past — the chief, noble one! A hundred leaves towards this past — that one — brilliant! Breast-cry — this past.

**Pr5–Pr6** — *Enumeration Continues*
> The path, the form, the life. Counting sticks and fruit. The world-cloud, the cave. I, I — on the path. Red flame, the brow of day.

**Pr7** — *The Counting-Stick Formula*
> Yes — strike, it is! Look towards the counting stick — shine! In [the breath] — breast-cry — the — [pause] — flee! Counting stick? — north wind! Counting stick — cradle — of rain — [pause] — life, form — bay and outrigger — hard? Life? Yes — red flame — path — like, as!

**Pr8–Pr11** — *Recto Closure*
> The form, the hundred leaves, the chief. Path and path-sun. The torch of dry leaves shines. Fruit, breast-cry, and the small. The counting sticks enumerate — here — life — yes! Flash of lightning towards the red.

### Verso (Pv1–Pv11)

**Pv1–Pv3** — *The Verso Opens*
> The path — it is him — brow of day. Here — breast-cry — look — it is him, there. The awakening. The chief — anger — rise and flee.

**Pv4–Pv6** — *Vision and Conflict*
> Awakening in the look. The intense grooves on the slopes. Path-sun, bloom. World-cloud. The angry chief — and with — rise! Flee! Yes — anger, yes! The form and the red flame, the sun.

**Pv7** — *The Double Death*
> The hawk — in — yes — that one — that one — come! Come! Die! Die! Red flame — in — you — fruit — who? — yes! Of yes! World-cloud — in.

**Pv8–Pv10** — *Toward Resolution*
> The sun, the shadow. Sacred power, the altar-platform. Bay and outrigger. The hundred leaves, the chief, the bloom. Singing and reciting — the path of the sun. The similar — the similar.

**Pv11** — *Closure*
> Sun — and with the path — shadow-spirit — yes! — the similar.

## 3. Philological Notes

### 3.1 Pr1 vs. Hr1 — Variant Openings

The openings of P and H are closely parallel but not identical:

| Element | H (Hr1) | P (Pr1) |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Initial word | *noma* (flash of lightning) | *i ~ noma* (in the flash of lightning) |
| Second phrase | *i rae i* (of the brow of day, in) | *hua-ora i* (result of the living, in) |
| Shared core | *rari... hara... anoenae... rau ahua araa* | *rari... rau... anoenae... rau ahua araa* |
| Closure | *au au... ura* (I, I... red flame) | *au i au... noma ki ura-rau* (I of I... lightning towards red leaf) |

The variant *hua-ora* ("result of the living" or "fruit of life") in P's opening where H has *rae* ("brow of day") is significant. It suggests the P scribe — or the P performance tradition — foregrounded the biological metaphor (life-fruit) while H foregrounded the celestial one (dawn-brow). Both are valid cosmogonic framings.

### 3.2 The *koara* Hapax

The sequence *koara* in Pr1 is unattested in Polynesian dictionaries. It could be a compound of *ko* (predicate marker) + *ara* (path/awake), yielding "it is the path" or "it is awakening." Alternatively, it may be an archaic ritual term lost from the modern lexicon. Compare *noanoa* (fragrant) and similar reduplicated forms in ritual Rapa Nui.

### 3.3 Pr7 — Rain, Cradle, and North Wind

Line Pr7 contains a remarkable cluster of meteorological and domestic vocabulary: *ute* (cradle), *ua* (rain), and a possible *tokerau* (north wind, here abbreviated or variant). This sequence is shared with tablet Q (where the "rain and cradle" motif gives Q its descriptive name). The juxtaposition of cradle and rain within a cosmological enumeration suggests a fertility passage: rain nourishes the earth as the cradle nourishes the child.

### 3.4 Pv7 — The Double Death

The most dramatic passage in the entire rongorongo corpus appears on Pv7:

> **mai mai mate mate ura** — Come! Come! Die! Die! Red [flame]!

The doubled imperative (*mai mai*) followed by the doubled death (*mate mate*) is unique across all tablets. The intensification through reduplication is a standard Polynesian rhetorical device (compare Hawaiian *make make* = "dying, dying" in funeral chants). The passage immediately following — *ura* (red/flame) — invokes the color of blood, fire, and sunset. This is an eschatological statement of maximum intensity: an invocation to come and witness death, bathed in red.

The hawk (*kahu*) that precedes this passage may represent a psychopomp — a spirit guide between the world of the living and the dead. In Polynesian mythology, birds frequently serve as messengers between *ao* (the world of light) and *po* (the underworld).

### 3.5 *Iraki* — A Rare Compound

The sequence *iraki* in Pv7 does not appear in standard dictionaries but may be analyzed as *ira* (skin, surface, mark) + *ki* (towards), yielding "towards the mark" or "at the sign." Alternatively, it could relate to *tiraki* (hoist, raise) with a dropped initial syllable — a common phenomenon in rapid ritual recitation.

### 3.6 The Parallel Structure H/P

Tablets H and P share the following structural template:

1. *Noma* opening (Hr1 / Pr1)
2. Enumerative stations with *rao* (Hr2–Hr7 / Pr2–Pr7)
3. *Arii... raaraa* passage (Hr5 / Pr4)
4. *Rao... rama* counting formula (Hr7 / Pr7)
5. Verso emotional intensification (Hv1–Hv11 / Pv1–Pv10)
6. *Mate* passage (absent in H / Pv7 — P's unique contribution)
7. *Rite* closure (Hv12 / Pv11)

The double death passage (Pv7) is P's distinctive addition to the shared template, absent or not preserved on H. This suggests that P represents a slightly expanded performance tradition that included an explicit eschatological section.

### 3.7 Coverage Differential

P's 91.7% coverage (vs. H's 93.6%) is due to a small number of glyphs that appear on P but not on the other Grand Texte copies. These are likely scribal variants or allographic choices specific to the St. Petersburg workshop tradition. The core vocabulary and structure are identical.

## 5. Interpretation

### 5.1 Genre and Relation to H

Tablet P is the second witness to the *Grand Texte*, the longest and most important liturgical text in the rongorongo corpus. It follows the same structural template as H — lightning opening, enumerative stations, angry chief, emotional verso, monistic closure — but with significant variants that reveal the text was a living liturgical composition, not a fixed scripture.

The most important variant is the **double death passage** (Pv7), which does not appear clearly on H. This suggests P preserves a performance tradition that included an explicit descent into death before the final resolution. If the Grand Texte narrates the cosmogonic cycle, Pv7 represents the nadir — the moment of maximum destruction before rebirth.

### 5.2 The Eschatological Section (Pv7)

The sequence *kahu... mai mai mate mate ura* (hawk... come, come, die, die, red) is the emotional and theological center of tablet P. Several observations:

1. **The hawk as psychopomp**: In Polynesian tradition, the *kahu* (hawk/harrier) is associated with the spirit world. Its appearance immediately before the death invocation positions it as a guide for the soul.

2. **Doubled imperatives**: The reduplication *mai mai* and *mate mate* is not merely emphatic but performative — the reciter is calling death into being. In Polynesian oral tradition, the doubled form has magical efficacy: saying a thing twice makes it real.

3. **Red flame closure**: The *ura* (red/flame) that follows death links this passage to the broader theme of transformation through fire. Death is not annihilation but combustion — a change of state.

4. **The address to "you"**: The pronoun *oe* (you) appears in this passage, unique in the Grand Texte tradition. The reciter is addressing the listener directly, breaking the cosmological frame to make the eschatological message personal: *you* will come, *you* will die.

### 5.3 P as the "Complete" Copy

If H is the longest copy and Q is the shortest, P may be the most thematically complete. It alone preserves:
- The variant *hua-ora* (fruit of life) opening
- The rain-cradle fertility passage (Pr7)
- The double death eschatology (Pv7)
- The *rite* closure shared with H

This makes P the preferred witness for understanding the full scope of the Grand Texte liturgy.

### 5.4 The St. Petersburg Provenance

Tablet P arrived in Russia through Nikolai Miklukho-Maklay, who collected it on Rapa Nui in the 1870s. The fact that this particular performance tradition (with the double death passage) left the island so early suggests it was still being actively inscribed and performed in the mid-19th century — consistent with historical reports that the last *tangata rongorongo* died in the 1860s–1870s.
