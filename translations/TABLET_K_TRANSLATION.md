## Philological Notes

### 1. Tablet K as a compressed Tablet G

The most striking feature of Tablet K is its near-exact correspondence with Tablet G's recto. The parallel sequences operate at the individual glyph level across extended passages:

| Tablet K | Tablet G | Shared content |
|----------|----------|---------------|
| Kr1--Kr3 | Gr1--Gr3 | Opening + sensory vocabulary |
| Kr4--Kr5 | Gr4--Gr5 | "ie!" litany + sun + "ieai" |
| Kv1--Kv3 | Gr5--Gr6 | "ei! raa" + look/smell + "yes!" cascade |

This is not approximate similarity -- the sequences match sign by sign. Tablet K is a **compressed excerpt** of the kai-kai portion of Tablet G, occupying 264 glyphs where G's recto uses approximately 416. The compression is achieved by shortening or omitting passages between the parallel blocks, not by abridging the blocks themselves.

Two explanations present themselves:
**(a)** K is a portable aide-memoire for a performer who already knew the complete text -- a "pocket version" of the full chant preserved on G.
**(b)** Both tablets copy from a common oral or written source, with K reproducing only the first half.

That K is the smallest classic tablet in the corpus supports interpretation (a): it is sized for portability rather than archival completeness.

### 2. Statistical significance -- p = 0.009

Tablet K achieves the highest individual significance of any tablet in leave-one-tablet-out cross-validation (p = 0.009, Z = 1.73). This is remarkable for the smallest classic tablet, with only approximately 40 decoded segments available for the permutation test. The strong signal reflects two factors:

**(a) High coverage (95.5%).** Almost every glyph is decoded, leaving very few unknowns to dilute the signal.

**(b) Concentrated vocabulary.** The kai-kai genre uses a compact set of high-frequency, well-attested words (ie, raa, ui, eo, rau, tu, ka) that are all in the dictionary. Unlike Tablet E's funerary register (which uses rare vocabulary and scores Z = -0.08), K's kai-kai register hits the dictionary at a consistently high rate.

The p = 0.009 result means: if the ALBA grid were random, there is less than a 1% chance that this many dictionary matches would occur on Tablet K. This is the single strongest per-tablet validation of the decipherment.

### 3. Kr1 -- "uuri" (dark)

The opening word "uuri" (dark, gloomy) is one of the early kill-shot confirmations of the grid: glyph 009 was assigned /u/ specifically because "uuri" emerged as the unique dictionary match in a constrained segment. Its position at the very start of the tablet sets a somber tone that contrasts with the ecstatic "ie!" litany to follow -- darkness before light, a common Polynesian rhetorical arc.

### 4. Kr2--Kr3 -- root, string, medicine

The pre-litany lines contain vocabulary that names the instruments and purpose of the kai-kai performance:
- **tui** (sew, string) -- directly references the string figures
- **aka** (root) -- foundation, origin; also the physical root fiber used in string-making
- **rao** (counting stick) -- a mnemonic device; its presence alongside "medicine" suggests a ritual counting sequence
- **raau** (medicine, remedy) -- healing vocabulary; the kai-kai may have had therapeutic functions

This cluster is self-referential: the chant declares what it is before performing what it does. String, root, counting stick, medicine -- the tools of the healer-performer laid out as a preamble.

### 5. Kr5 and the "ieai" compound

Line Kr5 contains the same "ieai" compound found in Gr4 and Gr6 (see Tablet G notes). Its appearance here confirms that "ieai" is a fixed formulaic expression of the kai-kai genre, not an ad hoc combination. The full sequence -- "ie! raa o ie! o ieai ie! au ie! ra" (yes! sun of yes! of ieai yes! I/me yes! there) -- is a crescendo: the performer affirms the sun, invokes the compound exclamation, declares "I/me" (personal participation), and closes with "there" (spatial grounding).

The first-person pronoun *au* (I/me) is rare in the corpus and signals that the performer steps into the text at this moment of maximum intensity.

### 6. Kv1 -- "ei! raa" (hey! sun)

The verso opens with the shortest, most direct address to the sun in the entire corpus: "ei! raa" -- a cry and a noun, two syllables. This may mark the midpoint of the performance where the string figure is completed and the performer calls out to the sun before the closing litany.

### 7. Kv2 -- sensory parallels with Gr6

Line Kv2 reproduces the "look... smell" (*ui... eo*) pairing from Gr6 nearly verbatim. This sensory vocabulary (looking and smelling) is characteristic of the kai-kai genre and may relate to the physical experience of the string-figure performance: looking at the completed figure, smelling the fiber or the smoke of nearby fires. The "oha" at the opening of the line may be *oha* (to sprout, to branch) -- a metaphor for the string figure spreading between the fingers.

### 8. Kv3 -- the "yes!" climax

Line Kv3 contains the densest concentration of "ie!" on the verso: "yes! [...] yes, yes! yes! leaf/hundred, yes! in -- yes!" The word *rau* (leaf/hundred) between the exclamations may indicate a count of string crossings or serve as a metaphor for the complexity of the figure being displayed.

### 9. Kv4--Kv5 -- abrupt ending

The tablet ends abruptly: Kv4 trails off with demonstratives and prepositions, and Kv5 consists of a single word ("of"). This is consistent with a text that was deliberately truncated -- the performer knew the rest by heart, and the tablet served only as a prompt for the opening. The demonstrative *eera* (that, emphatic) in Kv4 may be a deictic gesture: "That one!" -- pointing to the completed string figure before the chant dissolves.

### 10. Unresolved lexemes

- **"uuri"** (Kr1): phonetically clear (/u-u-ri/), semantically "dark/gloomy" (Englert) -- could also be a proper name
- **"oiio"** (Kr2): not in dictionary; possibly *oi-io* (cry-hawk?) or a variant exclamation
- **"koara"** (Kr3): not attested; possibly *ko-ara* (it is the path) fused, or a proper name
- **"rio"** (Kr3): possibly *rio* = to shine, gleam (Tahitian cognate)
- **"oha"** (Kv2): possibly *oha* = to sprout, branch (attested in broader Polynesian)

## Interpretation

Tablet K is a **kai-kai** (string-figure chant) -- the smallest classic tablet in the corpus but statistically the most significant validation of the ALBA grid (p = 0.009). Its near-exact parallelism with Tablet G's recto establishes the kai-kai as a fixed liturgical genre with standardized text, not an improvised performance.

### The kai-kai as genre

The Rapa Nui *kai-kai* is well documented ethnographically (Blixen 1979, Campbell 1971, Routledge 1919). Performers manipulated string figures while reciting rhythmic chants, with the audience responding in unison. The "ie!" exclamation that dominates Tablets K and G(recto) is the written trace of this audience participation -- a transcription of collective voice.

Routledge recorded that certain string figures were sacred and could only be performed at specific times. The figures themselves were mnemonic: each configuration encoded a narrative element (a deity, an event, a genealogical link). The tablet is therefore not simply a text to be read but a **performance score**: the glyphs encode both the words of the chant and the rhythm of the collective response.

### Structure of the chant

The text divides into three sections:

1. **Naming the instruments** (Kr1--Kr3): darkness, string, root, counting stick, medicine. The chant begins by declaring its own tools and purpose -- a self-referential opening common in Polynesian oral literature. The word *tui* (sew/string) is the genre marker, announcing that what follows is a string-figure text.

2. **The ecstatic litany** (Kr4--Kr5, Kv1--Kv3): the "ie!" refrain erupts. The sun is invoked. The senses are engaged (look, smell). The performer declares "I/me" (*au*) -- personal presence within the ritual. This is the core of the kai-kai: the moment when the string figure is displayed and the audience responds.

3. **The dissolve** (Kv4--Kv5): the text trails off into demonstratives and prepositions, ending with a single word. The written text has served its purpose as a prompt; the rest is performed from memory. The demonstrative "eera" (that one!) may be the final deictic gesture -- the performer pointing to the completed figure.

### The K/G doublet

The near-identity of K with G(recto) means we have **two independent witnesses** to this kai-kai text. Tablet G is larger and continues the chant beyond where K ends (Gr7--Gr8 contain additional material), and the G verso carries an entirely different text (a mana chant). This confirms that the kai-kai and the mana chant were separable liturgical units -- K copies only the invocation, not the theological meditation.

The existence of multiple copies at different scales is analogous to the relationship between a full liturgical book and a pocket breviary in medieval European tradition. The rongorongo scribal system maintained both archival tablets (G) and portable performance copies (K).

### The therapeutic dimension

The presence of *raau* (medicine/remedy) and *aka* (root) in the pre-litany section (Kr3) suggests that this kai-kai had a **healing function**. Therapeutic string-figure chants are attested across Polynesia: the rhythmic manipulation of string, combined with vocal invocation and audience participation, was understood as a mechanism for channeling mana toward a patient. The darkness (*uuri*) that opens the chant may describe the patient's condition; the sun (*raa*) that dominates the litany may describe the desired outcome. The chant moves from darkness to light, from illness to healing.

### Significance for decipherment

Despite its small size, Tablet K provides three critical contributions:

1. **Statistical anchor**: p = 0.009 is the strongest per-tablet validation in the corpus. The ALBA grid produces more dictionary matches on K than 99.1% of random grids.

2. **Genre confirmation**: Together with G(recto), K establishes kai-kai as a distinct rongorongo genre with its own structural grammar (refrain punctuation, sensory vocabulary, self-referential opening).

3. **Textual transmission**: The K/G doublet demonstrates that rongorongo texts were copied and transmitted -- the tablets are not unique compositions but part of a scribal tradition that maintained multiple copies of important texts.
