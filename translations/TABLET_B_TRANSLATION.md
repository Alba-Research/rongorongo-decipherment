## Philological Notes

### 1. Genre Classification: Composite Patautau

Tablet B is classified as a *patautau* (ritual recitation) in the ALBA genre taxonomy, alongside tablets N and A. However, it differs from the relatively uniform solar hymns of N (the "raaraa" tablet) in its composite structure. The text weaves together at least four distinct thematic strands:

- **Rain liturgy** (ua, dominant on recto): Br1, Br7, Br9, Bv3, Bv9, Bv12
- **Sugarcane/fertility cycle** (to, tu): Br4--Br6, Bv2
- **Sleep and silence** (moe, muteki): Br3--Br4
- **Solar affirmation** (raa, ura): Br10, Bv4, Bv5, Bv8--Bv9

This composite character suggests Tablet B is a liturgical anthology -- multiple short recitations inscribed on a single tablet, possibly representing a seasonal cycle or a sequence of ceremonies.

### 2. The Rain Theme

The word *ua* (rain) appears approximately 12 times across the tablet, making it the dominant semantic element after the universal *raa* (sun). Rain is particularly concentrated on the recto (Br1, Br7, Br9) and in the closing verso lines (Bv9, Bv12). In Polynesian cosmology, rain is the union of Rangi (sky-father) and Papa (earth-mother); its prominence here is consistent with a fertility recitation.

The phrase "rain [...] form/image [...] throw with both hands" (Br9) suggests a ritual gesture -- the officiant casting an offering into rain or mimicking the scattering of seed. The word *akiri* (throw with both hands) appears in ethnographic descriptions of Rapa Nui planting ceremonies.

### 3. The Sugarcane Motif

Sugarcane (*to*) appears 5 times (Br4, Br5 x2, Bv2, and possibly Br6). In Rapa Nui culture, sugarcane was a prestige crop and one of the few plants cultivated before European contact. The juxtaposition of *to* (sugarcane), *tu* (stand), and *au* (I/me) in Br5 reads as a first-person declaration: "Sugarcane stands: I breathe!" This may be a planting chant -- the speaker identifies with the planted cane, asserting vitality.

The pairing of *to* (sugarcane) with *mate* (die) in Bv2 introduces a life-death polarity centered on the agricultural cycle: the cane lives, the cane dies, and fruit (*hua*) follows.

### 4. Sleep and Silence (Br3--Br4)

The unique cluster *muteki muteki moe / moe amo* (be quiet, be quiet, sleep / sleep, carry) in Br3--Br4 has no parallel in other tablets. The word *muteki* (keep quiet, be silent) is rare in the corpus. This passage may represent a lullaby fragment or a ritual invocation of dormancy -- perhaps the "sleeping" phase of a planted field before germination.

### 5. The Ceremonial Strike (Br6)

The phrase *ta tea... tahua ta ko* (strike the light/white... ceremonial place, strike, it is) combines two ritual terms: *ta* (strike, beat -- as in drumming or percussive ritual) and *tahua* (ceremonial place, *marae*). The adjective *tea* (white, light, fair) may refer to a white offering or to dawn light. This line may describe the beginning of a ceremony at the *tahua* with percussive invocation.

### 6. The Hawk Motif (Br8, Bv12)

The hawk (*io*) appears twice, at Br8 (recto) and Bv12 (verso closing). In Polynesian tradition, the hawk/kite is a messenger between the human and divine realms. Its placement at the end of both sides of the tablet suggests a framing device -- the hawk carries the recitation upward.

### 7. Triple Shining (Bv9)

The phrase *rarara* (shine x3) in Bv9 echoes the famous *raaraa raaraa raaraa* refrain of Tablet N (Na1). However, Bv9 places it within a rain context: "Rain. Shine, shine, shine! [...] A cradle of the sun." This fusion of rain and radiance -- the sun shining through rain -- is a powerful image in Polynesian poetry, often associated with rainbows as divine bridges.

### 8. Medicine and Counting (Bv10--Bv12)

The final three lines introduce vocabulary absent from the rest of the tablet: *rao* (counting stick), *raau* (medicine/remedy), and *keri* (dig). The *rao* is an ethnographically attested Rapa Nui mnemonic device -- a stick with notches used for counting and record-keeping. Its appearance alongside *raau* (herbal medicine) and *keri* (dig, as in digging roots) suggests a pharmacological register: a recipe or preparation ritual for plant-based remedies. This supports the composite classification -- the scribe included a medicinal chant alongside the rain and sugarcane liturgy.

### 9. Structural Observations

- **Recto-verso asymmetry**: The recto is more coherent and liturgical (rain cycle, sugarcane fertility). The verso is more fragmented, with shorter thematic units and more emotional interjections (*aue*, *aa*, *e*).
- **First-person voice**: *au* (I/me) appears twice (Br5, Bv3), both times in active declarations. This is unusual -- most tablets use impersonal or third-person constructions. Tablet B may preserve a priest's personal invocation.
- **Exclamatory density**: The verso contains an unusually high density of affirmative particles: *aa* (yes!), *e* (hey!), *aue* (alas!). This is consistent with a performed recitation with audience responses.
- **The hawk frame**: *io* (hawk) at Br8 and Bv12 may mark structural divisions, functioning as a section delimiter comparable to glyph 200 (the taxogramme word-separator) but at a higher textual level.

## Interpretation: A Composite Liturgical Anthology

### Overall Structure

Tablet B (Aruku Kurenga) is a **composite *patautau*** -- a ritual recitation tablet containing multiple thematic sections inscribed as a continuous text. Unlike the relatively homogeneous solar hymn of Tablet N or the funerary narrative of Tablet E, Tablet B assembles at least four distinct liturgical registers into a single artifact:

| Section | Lines | Theme | Key vocabulary |
|---------|-------|-------|---------------|
| I. Rain Invocation | Br1--Br2 | Life through rain | ua, ora, mau, rae |
| II. Sleep/Germination | Br3--Br4 | Silence, dormancy, planting | moe, amo, to |
| III. Sugarcane Fertility | Br5--Br6 | Growth, standing, ceremonial strike | to, tu, au, ta, tahua |
| IV. Rain Liturgy | Br7--Br9 | Scattering, naming, offering | ua, hua, umu, ute |
| V. Solar Affirmation | Br10--Bv2 | Sun, fire, life-death axis | raa, ura, ora, mate |
| VI. Joy and Lament | Bv3--Bv6 | Altar, cave, singing, alas | ahu, ana, ako, koa, aue |
| VII. The Path of the Sun | Bv7--Bv9 | Movement, shining, cradle | ou, rarara, oraa, raa |
| VIII. Medicine/Closing | Bv10--Bv12 | Remedy, counting, digging | raau, rao, keri, io |

### Thematic Synthesis

The tablet describes an **agricultural-ceremonial cycle**, from planting to harvest, framed by rain and sun:

1. **Opening** (Br1): The reciter invokes true life (*mau ora*) and rain (*ua*), establishing the two forces needed for growth. The repeated phrase "brow of day" (*rae*) places the ceremony at dawn.

2. **Planting** (Br3--Br5): The sequence sleep-silence-sugarcane traces the arc from seed dormancy through germination. The first-person declaration "I breathe!" (*au nga*) marks the moment of sprouting -- the speaker becomes the plant.

3. **Ceremony** (Br6--Br7): The reciter performs ritual actions at the *tahua* (ceremonial place): striking, shaking, naming. The invocation to "give a name" (*haakoakoa*) may refer to consecrating the field or the crop.

4. **Offering** (Br9): The central line of the tablet, rich with concrete nouns: conch, rain, fruit, cradle, cooking pit. This reads as a list of offerings or ritual implements used in the ceremony.

5. **Affirmation** (Br10): The recto closes with a triple conjunction (*ma ma ma* -- "and with, and with, and with") leading to the sun. This formulaic repetition is characteristic of Polynesian chant intensification.

6. **Death and Rebirth** (Bv1--Bv2): The verso opens with weeping and death (*mate*), but immediately returns to fruit (*hua*) and affirmation (*aa*). The agricultural reading: the cane is cut (dies), but bears fruit.

7. **Joy in the Cave** (Bv3): The most vivid passage: "World, joy! Sing in the cave." (*ao koa ako ana*). Caves in Rapa Nui (*ana*) were sacred spaces for initiation and recitation. This may describe the performance context of the chant itself.

8. **The Triple Shining** (Bv9): The climactic moment of the verso -- *rarara* (shine x3) -- reprises the solar radiance theme of the *patautau* genre. The "cradle of the sun" (*ute raa*) may denote the horizon at sunrise.

9. **Closing: Medicine and the Hawk** (Bv10--Bv12): The tablet ends with a pharmacological register (counting sticks, remedies, digging for roots) and the hawk (*io*) as final image. The hawk carries the ceremony to its completion, ascending with the prayer.

### Cultural Context

The composite nature of Tablet B is consistent with its traditional name, *Aruku Kurenga*, which Barthel tentatively linked to a type of ceremonial recitation. The mixture of agricultural, liturgical, and medicinal content on a single tablet suggests it functioned as a **priestly handbook** -- a collection of chants needed for different moments of a seasonal festival cycle, inscribed together for convenience.

The prominence of sugarcane (*to*) is notable. Unlike the taro and yam that dominate Polynesian agriculture elsewhere, sugarcane held special status on Rapa Nui as one of the few crops that could grow in the island's thin volcanic soil. A chant cycle centered on sugarcane planting, growth, harvest, and renewal would have been culturally significant.

The presence of first-person voice (*au*), direct address (*oe*), exclamatory particles (*aa*, *aue*, *e*), and the cave performance reference (Bv3) all point to a text designed for **oral performance with audience participation** -- the *tangata rongorongo* reciting while an audience responds with affirmations.

### Confidence Assessment

- **High confidence**: Genre classification (composite *patautau*), presence of rain/sugarcane/sun themes, structural division between recto fertility cycle and verso death-rebirth-joy arc.
- **Medium confidence**: Individual word readings (93.3% coverage means ~109 glyphs remain undecoded), the agricultural-ceremonial interpretation, hawk as structural frame.
- **Low confidence**: Specific ritual actions described, the pharmacological reading of Bv10--Bv12, identification of *enana* as "people" (Marquesan loanword or coincidence).
