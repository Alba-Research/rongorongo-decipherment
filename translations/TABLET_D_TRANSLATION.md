## 2. Philological Notes

### Da3: The fivefold "ki" (towards)

Line Da3 is the rhythmic backbone of the tablet. The word *ki* (towards, direction) appears five times in succession after a doubled *ra* (there). This is not a scribal repetition but a performative structure: in Polynesian oral tradition, directional repetition marks the opening of sacred space. The fivefold *ki* evokes the five cardinal points of Polynesian cosmology (four winds plus the zenith), or the five stages of a cosmic journey. Parallel structures exist in Maori *karakia* (invocations) where directional words are chanted in repetition to establish the axis of ritual.

### Da4: "kaki" and the Pleiades

The word *kaki* in Da4 is read as the Pleiades cluster (Matariki in Rapa Nui, though the archaic form varies). The Pleiades mark the beginning of the agricultural/ceremonial year across Polynesia. Their appearance in a cosmogonic text between two references to the "brow of day" (*rae*) places them as a waypoint on the sun's descent -- the sun passes through the stars on its way down. The imperative *ka* (let it be!) following the stellar reference suggests a command to the heavens.

### Da5: "emu" (to sink/founder)

The verb *emu* (to sink, to founder, to be submerged) at the end of Da5 marks the turning point of the cosmogony. After the solar descent through lines Da1--Da4, after the triple affirmation *i, i, i* (in, within) and the exclamation *ei* (hey!), the sun physically sinks. This is the moment of solar death -- the transition from recto (upper world) to verso (lower world). The verb choice is significant: *emu* in Rapa Nui denotes specifically a downward submersion, not mere disappearance.

### Da8: "oe" (you!) as dramatic pivot

Line Da8 contains a single word: *oe* (you). This is the most dramatic moment in the tablet. After the sun has sunk (Da5) and the narrative has thinned to near-silence (Da6--Da7), the text addresses someone directly. In Polynesian chant, the sudden shift to second person marks a ritual interpellation -- the dead person, the listener, or the initiate is called upon. The isolation of this word on an entire line amplifies its force. It functions as the hinge between the cosmic narrative (recto) and the funerary rite (verso): the sun has descended, and now *you* must follow.

### Db2: "ura" (red/flame) framing

The word *ura* (red, flame, glow) appears in both Db2 and Db6 -- the first and last substantive lines of the funerary section. This framing is deliberate. Red (*ura*) is the color of death and sacred power throughout Polynesia. In Rapa Nui, corpses were wrapped in red-stained *tapa* cloth, and the *ahu* (altars) were painted with red ochre. The fire brackets the funerary passage: the dead enter through flame and arrive in flame.

### Db4: "mu" (silence) and "rao" (counting stick)

The word *mu* (silence, to be silent) opens the funerary preparation. Silence in Polynesian ritual is not absence but presence -- it is the *tapu* (sacred restriction) that descends over a death ceremony. The *rao* (counting stick) at the end of Db4 is a ritual implement: a notched stick used to count days of mourning, offerings, or genealogical generations. Its appearance here suggests the formal accounting that accompanies death -- the measuring of the deceased's place in the cosmic order. The juxtaposition of silence and counting embodies the paradox of funerary ritual: speech ceases, but measurement continues.

### Db5: "umu" (cooking pit), "rana" (obsidian point)

Two words in Db5 are specifically funerary. The *umu* (earth oven, cooking pit) is the ceremonial pit where offerings are cooked for the dead -- in Rapa Nui, the *umu pare haonga* was a funerary feast oven. The *rana* (obsidian point) is the sharp volcanic glass used for ritual scarification during mourning: mourners cut themselves to draw blood as an offering to the deceased. Together, these two objects -- the pit for the dead and the blade for the living -- form the material vocabulary of a Rapa Nui funeral.

### Db6: "pera" (cemetery/taboo place)

The final destination. *Pera* denotes a place of burial or taboo restriction. The sequence *ara ra pera ura* (path, there, cemetery, red flame) is the most compact summary of the entire tablet's cosmogonic arc: the path of the sun leads to the place of the dead, marked by red fire. The text ends not with closure but with color -- *ura* as the last word, leaving the flame burning.

## 4. Interpretation: Cosmogony as Funerary Liturgy

### 4.1 Overall structure

Tablet D is a **rongorongo** in the strict Rapa Nui sense -- a creation chant that maps the solar cycle onto the journey of the dead. The tablet divides into three acts:

| Act | Lines | Content | Movement |
|-----|-------|---------|----------|
| I. Solar Descent | Da1--Da4 | Sun shines, path opens, stars appear | Downward |
| II. The Sinking | Da5--Da8 | Affirmation, submersion, direct address | Pivot |
| III. Funerary Close | Db1--Db6 | Red flame, silence, instruments of death, cemetery | Arrival |

The recto-to-verso transition (Da8 to Db1) coincides with the physical act of turning the tablet over -- a performative enactment of the passage between worlds.

### 4.2 Da3: Rhythmic backbone

The fivefold *ki* in Da3 is the structural spine of the text. It establishes directionality -- the entire tablet moves *towards* something. On the recto, that something is the setting sun. On the verso, it is the cemetery. The repetition is not redundant but accumulative: each *ki* builds momentum for the descent. This matches the Polynesian chant technique of *tauparapara* (rhythmic preamble), where directional words are repeated to orient the spiritual force of the chant.

### 4.3 Da8: "You!" as ritual pivot

The single word *oe* (you) on Da8 transforms the text from narrative to address. Up to this point, the tablet describes cosmic events in the third person. With *oe*, the dead person is spoken to directly. This is the moment of ritual engagement -- the transition from cosmogony (describing how the world works) to liturgy (acting upon a specific person within that world). The brevity is the point: after 7 lines of cosmic architecture, one word collapses the distance between the universal and the individual.

### 4.4 Db4--Db6: The funerary toolkit

The final three readable lines of the verso assemble a precise inventory of death ritual:

1. **Silence** (*mu*) -- the *tapu* that falls over the ceremony
2. **Counting stick** (*rao*) -- the tally of mourning days or genealogical reckoning
3. **Cooking pit** (*umu*) -- the earth oven for funerary offerings
4. **Obsidian point** (*rana*) -- the blade for ritual scarification
5. **Cemetery** (*pera*) -- the final destination
6. **Red flame** (*ura*) -- the sacred fire that marks the boundary

This is not metaphor but material liturgy. Each word names an object or place that would have been physically present at a Rapa Nui funeral. The text is a performative checklist: as each word is chanted, the corresponding element of the ceremony is invoked or activated.

### 4.5 The "ura" frame

The word *ura* (red/flame) appears at Db2 and Db6, bracketing the entire funerary section. Red is the color of *mana* (sacred power), blood, and the afterlife in Polynesian culture. The framing creates a ritual enclosure: everything between the two fires belongs to the domain of the dead. This structural use of color-words as textual boundaries is also attested in the Mamari calendar (Tablet C), where *ura* marks transitions between lunar phases.

### 4.6 Cosmogonic logic

The tablet's deepest argument is an equivalence: **the sun's daily descent = the soul's final journey**. The same path (*ara*) that the sun takes from zenith (Da1: shining, piercing) through the stars (Da4: Pleiades) to submersion (Da5: sinking) is the path the dead person takes from life through ritual (Db4--Db5: silence, counting, sacrifice) to the cemetery (Db6). The text does not merely describe this parallel -- it enacts it. By chanting the solar descent on the recto and the funerary rite on the verso, the performer literally turns the world over, carrying the dead from one side to the other.

This places Tablet D squarely in the *rongorongo* genre alongside Tablets R and S: creation chants that use cosmological structure as the framework for ritual action. The distinguishing feature of D is its compression -- at 332 glyphs, it is the shortest of the three, and the most tightly organized around the single arc of descent.
