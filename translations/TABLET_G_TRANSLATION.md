## Philological Notes

### 1. The biface structure -- two genres on one tablet

Tablet G is the only tablet in the corpus with clearly distinct genres on each face. The recto (Gr1--Gr8) is a **kai-kai** (invocation/string-figure chant) dominated by the exclamation "ie!" (yes!), while the verso (Gv1--Gv8) is a **mana chant** centered on sacred power, fire, and theological vocabulary. This biface organization was first noted in the genre classification and is confirmed by word-frequency analysis: "ie" accounts for 21+ tokens on the recto but nearly disappears on the verso, where "mana," "rama," "rahu," and "ura" take over.

This suggests the tablet was inscribed on two separate occasions, or that the two faces represent two movements of a single ceremony -- the ecstatic invocation followed by the theological meditation.

### 2. Gr3--Gr6 -- the "yes!" litany

The four central recto lines form one of the most distinctive passages in the corpus. Nearly every semantic unit is punctuated by "ie!" (yes!), creating a relentless call-and-response rhythm:

> ... ie! uu, ngu, ie! [...] ie! [...] ie! ... ie!

This is the textual fingerprint of the *kai-kai* genre: rhythmic chants performed with string figures, where the audience or a chorus responds "ie!" after each line. The same pattern appears in compressed form on Tablet K (Kr4--Kr5, Kv1--Kv3), confirming a shared genre.

The vocabulary within the litany includes bodily and emotional terms: *uu* (breast/cry), *ngu* (moan), *ui* (look), *eo* (smell), *nua* (mother) -- sensory and maternal imagery framed by ecstatic affirmation.

### 3. Gr4 -- "ieai" as compound exclamation

The form "ieai" appears twice (Gr4, Gr6) and is not in the dictionary as a single lexeme. It likely represents the compound **ie + ai** (yes! + who/by) -- an emphatic "yes, by whom!" or simply an intensified exclamation with the interrogative particle fused into the cry. Alternatively, it may be a variant spelling of the exclamation chain "ie, ai!" preserved as a single phonetic unit in the oral tradition.

### 4. Gv4 -- the theological core

Line Gv4 contains the densest concentration of theological vocabulary in the corpus:
- **maa** (food) -- ritual offering
- **ea** (rise) -- ascension, emergence
- **mana** (sacred power) -- the central concept
- **mau** (true, fixed) -- permanence
- **hua** (fruit) -- abundance, result
- **ute** (cradle) -- origin
- **rama** (torch, dry leaves) -- sacred fire
- **rahu** (coal) -- embers of the sacred fire
- **ura** (red/flame) -- the color of mana

The sequence "maa ea o mana... mau" (food rises of sacred power... true/fixed) articulates a Polynesian liturgical statement: the offering ascends as mana, and mana is permanent. The fire vocabulary (torch, coal, red flame) places this in the context of a fire ceremony, consistent with the Rapa Nui *umu hanga* (earth oven ritual) or the lighting of ceremonial fires on the *ahu* platforms.

### 5. "ma" x43 -- conjunction or sacred?

The preprint notes that "ma" appears 43 times on Tablet G. Most occurrences function as the conjunction "and/with" (standard Rapa Nui *ma*), but the sheer frequency is abnormal. In Polynesian theology, *ma* also carries connotations of purity and cleanliness (cf. Maori *ma* = white, clean). The high frequency may reflect a deliberate double meaning: "and" on the grammatical level, "purity" on the ritual level.

### 6. Hawk (io) -- prestige bird

The word *io* (hawk, Circus approximans) in Gr5 is significant. In Maori tradition, *io* is the supreme deity; in broader Polynesian culture, the hawk is a messenger between the human and divine worlds. Its appearance in the litany alongside "cry/bawl" and "stand" may describe the hawk as a divine witness or intermediary.

### 7. Unresolved lexemes

- **"hui"** (Gr5): possibly *hui* = to assemble, gather (Polynesian cognate)
- **"iai"** (Gr5): variant of "ie" or a distinct form; possibly *iai* = to exist (Maori cognate)
- **"maio"** (Gv7): not attested; possibly *ma-io* (with-hawk) or a proper name
- **"eai"** (Gv7): variant exclamation, cf. "ieai" in Gr4

## Interpretation

Tablet G is a **liturgical diptych** -- two distinct but related ritual texts inscribed on the two faces of a single wooden tablet.

### The Recto: Ecstatic Invocation

The recto is a **kai-kai** (string-figure chant), a genre well attested in Rapa Nui ethnography. Kai-kai were performed by specialists who manipulated string figures while reciting rhythmic chants, with the audience responding "ie!" (yes!) at cadential points. The recto of Tablet G preserves this call-and-response structure in writing: the relentless "ie!" after each phrase is not an authorial choice but a transcription of the audience's participation.

The sensory vocabulary (look, smell, breast, moan, mother) combined with the hawk and the sun suggests an invocation to awaken the divine through embodied experience -- seeing, smelling, crying out, standing. The mother (*nua*) may refer to the Earth Mother or to a specific ancestral figure.

### The Verso: Sacred Fire Theology

The verso shifts to a meditative, theological register. The fire vocabulary (torch, coal, red flame) and the concept of mana (sacred power) dominate. The core statement in Gv4 -- food rises as sacred power, true and fixed -- articulates the central Polynesian theological proposition: mana is real, permanent, and transmitted through ritual offerings (especially fire and food).

The closing imagery of Gv7--Gv8 (awakening, rain, rising) suggests a cosmological cycle: the sacred fire awakens mana, which rises as rain (returning to the earth), and rises again. This cyclical theology is consistent with the broader Polynesian understanding of mana as a force that circulates between the human and divine worlds.

### The Diptych as Ceremony

Read as a unified ceremony, the tablet moves from ecstatic invocation (recto) to theological reflection (verso): first the community cries out in affirmation, then the priest meditates on the meaning of what has been invoked. This two-part structure -- participation followed by contemplation -- is a widespread pattern in Polynesian ritual.

The biface structure also explains the dual genre classification in the preprint: G(recto) = kai-kai, G(verso) = mana chant. These are not contradictory labels but complementary halves of a single liturgical event.
