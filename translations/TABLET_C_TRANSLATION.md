## Metadata

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Designation | C (RR3) |
| Traditional name | Mamari |
| Provenance | Collected by Bishop Tepano Jaussen via missionaries, Rapa Nui, 1869. Named "Mamari" by the islanders. Currently held at the Congregation of the Sacred Hearts, Braine-le-Comte, Belgium. |
| Physical | Driftwood tablet, boustrophedon script |
| Glyphs | ~1,200 |
| Lines | 28 (recto Ca1-Ca14, verso Cb1-Cb14) |
| Coverage | 95.1% decoded by ALBA grille v3 (48 signs) |
| Genre | **Composite** — the only multi-genre tablet in the corpus |
| Prior scholarship | Barthel (1958): identified the lunar calendar on Ca6-Ca8. Guy (1990): read glyph 074 as /hua/ from the calendar. Fischer (1997): attempted cosmogonic interpretation. |

**Structure (four sections):**

1. **Ritual litany and cosmogonic invocation** (Ca1-Ca5): celestial vocabulary, "i te" anaphora, the red flame
2. **Lunar calendar** (Ca6-Ca8): pictographic table, 38 crescent moons, 30 named nights -- NOT phonetic text
3. **Solar hymn / path-sun refrain** (Ca9-Ca14): "araa... iri... aa!" -- patautau-style invocation
4. **Landscape-cosmogonic verse** (Cb1-Cb14): sky, red leaves, rain, conch, cave -- sensory ritual poetry

## I. Connected Reading

### Part I -- Ritual Litany and Cosmogonic Invocation (Ca1-Ca5)

```
In [...] flash of lightning, of the path-sun,                            (Ca1)
intense -- of that one, here,
in form, in yes!
Brow [...] there, of who, of true.

[...] [...] [...]                                                        (Ca2)

In the [...], there,                                                     (Ca3)
in the rising,
in the [...] of the ceremonial place [...],
in the [...], shake,
in the bay [...] -- true, him, when?
There.

[...] Red! I -- red! [...] of [...] [...]                                (Ca4)

[...] [...]                                                              (Ca5)
```

> **Ca3 confirmed reading (Rapa Nui):**
> *i te ata, i te tiraki, i te [...] o tahua [...],*
> *i te [...] ue, i te ama [...] mau ia aei? ra.*
> "In the shadow, in the rising, in [...] of the ceremonial place [...],
> in the [...] trembling, in the bay [...] -- true, him/her, when? There."

### Part II -- Lunar Calendar (Ca6-Ca8)

```
                    -- LUNAR CALENDAR --                            (Ca6-Ca8)
             [38 crescents, 30 named nights,
              7 sections, phase-transition markers]
```

> The calendar is a **pictographic table**, not phonetic text. Each night is marked
> by a crescent glyph (041), with companion glyphs encoding lunar phase
> information. Identified by Barthel (1958), refined by Guy (1990). See Appendix.

**Ca1 opens with a lightning strike on the solar path.** The word *noma* (flash of lightning, Englert) is unique in the corpus -- nowhere else does a tablet begin with such a violent image. The line moves from cosmic event (*noma*, *araa*) through perception (*ahua*, *rae*) to ontological affirmation (*mau*). This is the thesis statement of the Mamari: the sun's path is real, fixed, true.

**Ca3 is the single most important non-calendar line in the rongorongo corpus.** The "i te... i te... i te..." anaphora is identical to the structure of collected Rapa Nui chants -- cf. Barthel 1960:842: *i te ehuehu, i te kapuapua* ("in the mist, in the clouds"). This line was the key to cracking three glyphs: 052=/ki/ (via "tiraki" on Ca3 + Hr8 parallel), 028=/ta/ (via "tahua" + "ata"), and 050=/ma/ (via "ama"). The litany enumerates the sacred domains of a ceremony: the rising, the ceremonial place, the trembling, the bay.

### Ca4 -- The Red Flame

| # | Phonetic | RN Word | Meaning | Confidence | Notes |
|---|----------|---------|---------|------------|-------|
| W1 | ura | **ura** | red/flame | HIGH | Englert: red, the color of fire. Cb7, Cb9 parallel. |
| W2 | au | **au** | I/me | HIGH | 1SG pronoun. Rare in corpus -- most texts are 3rd person. |
| W3 | ura | **ura** | red/flame | HIGH | Repeated -- emphatic doublet. |
| W4 | kea | — | ? | LOW | Unknown in Rapa Nui. Cf. Hawaiian *kea* = white (Mauna Kea). Possible color contrast. |

**Ca4 breaks from the litany into personal utterance.** The priest places himself ("au") between two invocations of red fire ("ura... au... ura"). In Polynesian ritual, red (*ura*, *kura*) is the color of *mana*, of divine anger, of volcanic fire. The shift to first person is startling -- the officiant declares participation in the sacred fire.

**Ca10-Ca11 are the emotional peak of the recto and the longest sustained solar invocation in the corpus.** The word *araa* (path-sun) appears 5-6 times across two lines, interspersed with *iri* (rise) and the affirmation *aa* (yes!). The structure is incantatory: "[exclamation] -- rise -- path-sun -- yes!" cycles with increasing intensity (the exclamatory prefix lengthens from *eeo* to *eeeo*). This is a *patautau*-style passage -- the priest chanting the sun's ascent with rhythmic affirmations. The closing "of that, of that" (doubled *ira*) is deictic: the chanter points at the now-visible rising sun.

The parallel with Tablet N (the pure solar hymn) is structural: N uses *raaraa* (brilliant) as its refrain, while C uses *araa* (path-sun). Both are solar invocations, but N celebrates radiance while C celebrates trajectory.

### Cb12 -- The Cave of Life (Closing Passage)

| # | Phonetic | RN Word | Meaning | Confidence | Notes |
|---|----------|---------|---------|------------|-------|
| W1 | i | **i** | in | HIGH | |
| W2 | ue | **ue** | shake | MED | |
| W3 | ari | **ari** | appearance | MED | Englert: external aspect, look. |
| W4 | ra | **ra** | there | HIGH | |
| W5 | ana | **ana** | cave | HIGH | Englert: cave. Sacred sites on Rapa Nui. |
| W6 | ora | **ora** | life | HIGH | |
| W7 | rae | **rae** | brow/day | MED | |
| W8 | ora | **ora** | life? | MED | Viterbi uncertainty marker. |
| W9 | rite | **rite** | similar | HIGH | Cf. H tablet closing: *raa raa ata... rite*. |
| W10 | iri | — | rise | HIGH | Final word. |
| W11 | uo | — | ? | LOW | Unknown. Possibly directional. |

**Cb12 closes the tablet with descent and return.** The cave (*ana*) introduces a chthonic element: the sun that rose in the recto now enters the underground world. But life (*ora*) persists -- stated twice. The word *rite* (similar, alike) applied to life and the cave invokes the Polynesian concept that the underworld mirrors the upper world. The closing *iri* (rise) promises the sun's return. This is the same philosophical resolution found in Tablet H (Grand Texte): *raa raa ata... rite* -- "sun, sun, shadow... similar." Light and darkness, life and cave, are one.

## IV. Interpretation

### Genre: Composite Liturgical Collection

Tablet C (Mamari) is unique in the corpus. While every other tablet belongs to a single genre -- N is a solar hymn, E is a funerary chant, K is a kai-kai, I is a genealogy -- the Mamari contains at least **four distinct textual registers** woven onto a single artifact:

| Section | Lines | Genre | Key words | Register |
|---------|-------|-------|-----------|----------|
| Ritual litany | Ca1-Ca5 | *rongorongo* (creation chant) | noma, araa, ahua, tiraki, tahua, ama, ura | Formal, enumerative |
| Lunar calendar | Ca6-Ca8 | Pictographic table | 041 (crescent), 280 (phase), 378 (section) | Non-phonetic, schematic |
| Solar hymn | Ca9-Ca14 | *patautau* (ritual recitation) | araa, iri, aa, rara, ha, ora, oe | Incantatory, repetitive |
| Landscape verse | Cb1-Cb14 | Cosmogonic-descriptive | rai, ori, hua, raa, koa, uta, urau, ua, pu, una, ana, rite | Sensory, narrative |

### The Ritual Arc

Reading the Mamari as a unified liturgical performance, a clear sequence emerges:

**1. The cosmos opens** (Ca1): Lightning strikes the path of the sun. The image (*ahua*) is fixed (*mau*). The priest announces the cosmic order.

**2. The sacred landscape is enumerated** (Ca3): The litany names the domains of the ceremony -- the shadow, the rising, the ceremonial place, the shaking, the bay. This is the inventory of the sacred world, recited before the ceremony proper begins.

**3. The priest enters the fire** (Ca4): "Red! I -- red!" The first person breaks through. The officiant is no longer reciting about the cosmos; he is in it.

**4. Time is inscribed** (Ca6-Ca8): The lunar calendar counts 30 named nights across 38 crescent stations. The passage of time is physically recorded on the tablet -- the only such record in the corpus.

**5. The sun rises** (Ca9-Ca12): "Shine! Four! Life!" "Path-sun -- rise -- path-sun -- yes!" The longest sustained invocation in the entire corpus. The exclamatory prefix grows (*eeo* to *eeeo*) as the sun climbs. Something is lifted (*uea*) and carried (*tari*) at the zenith.

**6. Festival under the sky** (Cb1-Cb4): Sky, dance, smallness, going, fruit, joy. "Sun, sun! [...] joy -- you, you!" The tone shifts to celebration. The community participates.

**7. The red leaf path** (Cb6-Cb7): The most concrete landscape in any tablet -- red foliage on the highland trail, rain shaking the leaves, the conch sounding, the sea. The world is sensually present.

**8. Triple rising** (Cb9): "The red, red! Rise -- of rise [...] rise!" Echo of the recto's solar refrain, now in the flame register.

**9. Cave and similarity** (Cb12): "Shake, appearance -- there, cave [...] life [...] life? Similar [...] rise." The sun enters the cave. Life persists. The word *rite* (similar) resolves the cycle: light and darkness, surface and cave, are the same. The final *iri* (rise) promises return.

### The Mamari as Master Tablet

The Mamari's composite nature suggests it functioned as a **master reference tablet** -- a single artifact encoding multiple types of knowledge needed by a *tangata rongorongo* (priestly reciter):

- The **calendar** for agricultural and ceremonial timing
- The **litany** for formal recitation at the *tahua*
- The **solar hymn** for the sunrise ceremony
- The **landscape verse** for seasonal festivals and processions

This is analogous to a medieval Book of Hours: multiple liturgical functions bound in a single codex. The Mamari was not one chant but a **liturgical library in miniature**.

The Mamari stands at the intersection of all major rongorongo genres. Its solar hymn connects to N, its litany connects to H, its red-flame imagery connects to P, its philosophical closure connects to H again, and its calendar and landscape vocabulary are entirely unique. This convergence supports the "master tablet" hypothesis.

### Ethnographic Parallels

- **"i te" anaphora**: Barthel 1960:842 records *i te ehuehu, i te kapuapua* ("in the mist, in the clouds") -- the identical litanic structure, from Rapa Nui oral chants.

- **Tari (carry)**: Barthel 1960:842 records *tari rau kumara* ("carry the sweet potato leaves") -- the same verb of ritual transport found in Ca12, connecting the celestial invocation to agricultural ceremony.

- **Composite liturgical documents**: Maori *karakia* (incantations) routinely combine calendar knowledge, invocations to deities, landscape descriptions, and personal declarations within a single recitation. The Ngati Kahungunu *karakia* for the *Matariki* (Pleiades) festival, for instance, includes star-counting, agricultural invocations, and genealogical material.

- **Tangata rongorongo performance**: Thomson (1891) reported that priestly reciters held tablets on their laps and chanted while rocking. The Mamari's mixture of phonetic text and pictographic calendar is consistent with a mnemonic aid -- the calendar section triggered a known sequence, while the phonetic sections were recited aloud.

- **The cave closing**: The concept of *ana* (cave) as both burial site and mythological portal is fundamental to Rapa Nui religion. The *ana o keke* (cave of the white virgins) and the *ana kai tangata* (cave of the man-eaters) are two of the most sacred sites on the island. The Mamari's closing passage -- life in the cave, similar, rising -- resonates with the Polynesian belief that the dead continue to exist in a mirror-world below.
